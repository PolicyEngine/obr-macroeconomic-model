# Calibration scorecard

How well does the **raw** model (add-factor residuals OFF) reproduce the OBR's
published baseline? The anchored model matches by construction, so the raw-vs-OBR
gap is the true measure of calibration. Generated by
`obr_macro/calibration_score.py` (raw model vs EFO, 2025Q1–2027Q4).

**The headline trap:** a `0.00%` error usually does **not** mean a good forecast
— it means the model left the variable at the OBR's published value because its
equation is dead or exogenous (*passthrough*). Only variables the model actually
**computes** measure calibration.

| Variable | Error (3yr) | Status |
|---|--:|---|
| Real GDP | 3.98% | computed — fair |
| Consumption | 6.63% | computed — fair |
| Business investment | 11.46% | computed — **poor** |
| Real household income | 12.84% | computed — **poor** |
| Employment | 0.00% | computed — trivial identity |
| Trade balance | 0.00% | computed — trivial identity |
| Investment, Exports, Imports, Employees, Avg earnings, CPI, RPI, CPI inflation, GDP deflator, Wages, Household income, Compensation, Company profits, Unemployment, Current account | 0.00% | **passthrough** (held at OBR value) |

## Honest score
- **6 of 21** headline variables are actually computed; the other **15 are passthrough**.
- 2 of the 6 are trivial identities; **4 are genuinely behavioural**, and those run
  **fair-to-poor (4–13% over 3 years)**.

## Verdict
Calibration is **partial and uneven**: decent on GDP shape and identities, fair on
consumption, poor on investment and real income, and absent on the ~15 dead
channels (trade, prices, labour demand, public finances). Consistent with the
data-coverage finding — a working core gated everywhere else by the unpublished
constants and deflator histories. Improving these scores is the same task as
reviving the dead channels, and is bounded by data the OBR does not publish.
